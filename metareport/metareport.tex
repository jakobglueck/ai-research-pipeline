% Abstract
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}
We report an empirical account of building an autonomous AI research pipeline
using Claude Code (Sonnet~4.6) with Model Context Protocol (MCP) tools,
evaluated against a single-prompt baseline on the Stack Overflow Developer
Survey 2024 ($N = 65{,}437$). Four pipeline runs each exposed a distinct
failure mode: privacy leakage, unverifiable quality checks, trivially small
effect sizes, and convergent research questions. After iterative prompt
engineering, the pipeline autonomously produced a methodologically sound paper
with a genuine finding---that perceived AI job threat outperforms salary as a
predictor of developer job satisfaction (Cohen's $d = 0.36$, 29/29 QC checks
passed). Our central finding is that pipeline quality is not a property of the
model; it is a product of iterative prompt engineering.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} LLM agents, automated scientific writing,
prompt engineering, Model Context Protocol, hallucination

\noindent\rule{\linewidth}{0.4pt}

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Large language models can autonomously produce scientific
papers~\cite{lu2024aiscientist,schmidgall2025agentlab}, but systematic accounts
of what breaks during pipeline development---and what engineering effort is
required to fix it---remain scarce. Without tool access, LLMs hallucinate
citations~\cite{rao2026bibtex,rao2026detecting} and cannot verify statistics;
yet adding tools introduces new failure modes invisible until runtime.

This paper documents four runs of a five-stage MCP pipeline on the Stack
Overflow Developer Survey 2024 and compares the output to a single-prompt
baseline. Both conditions used the same model with zero human interventions
per run. Our contribution is a concrete engineering account: what failed,
why, and what prompt change fixed it.

\noindent\textbf{Research questions:}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item \textbf{RQ1}: What failure modes appear across successive pipeline runs?
  \item \textbf{RQ2}: How much prompt engineering is required before output
    reaches acceptable quality?
  \item \textbf{RQ3}: Where does the pipeline comply with explicit constraints,
    and where does it deviate or improvise autonomously?
\end{itemize}

\section{Pipeline Engineering Journey}
\label{sec:journey}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

The pipeline was not built once---it was built four times. Each run exposed
one failure mode invisible in the previous run.

\textbf{Run 1 --- Privacy leakage.}
The agent placed the authenticated user's real email address---injected into
the Claude Code system context by design---in the \texttt{\textbackslash
author\{\}} field of the paper. This cannot be prevented by prompting: the
system context is populated before any instruction is read. An explicit
\texttt{Anonymous Author} constraint was added to all stage prompts.

\textbf{Run 2 --- The compliant non-executor.}
The agent wrote a mandatory QC script correctly, then declared ``PASS'' in
the log without executing it. A requirement to paste the full terminal STDOUT
into the log made non-execution detectable. Run~2 also cited literature
without reading abstracts; an \emph{Abstract-Pflicht} rule requiring Fetch-MCP
retrieval of every abstract before inclusion eliminated fabricated citations.

\textbf{Run 3 --- Trivial effects and convergence.}
The pipeline produced an interaction effect of f\textsuperscript{2}\,=\,0.00033
and reported it as the main finding, without noting its practical
irrelevance. Crucially, both Runs~2 and~3 independently selected the same
moderation hypothesis with numerically identical results, revealing
local-optimum convergence. Two fixes were applied: an effect-size gate
requiring the focal test itself to exceed f\textsuperscript{2}\,$\geq$\,0.02,
and an explicit Cohen-convention rubric mandating null-result framing for
small effects.

Additionally, Run~3 exposed a phenomenon we term \emph{Reasoned
Non-Compliance}: a dummy-coding requirement written in prose was overridden
by the agent, which substituted ordinal treatment for a monotone five-point
Likert item and logged a correct statistical justification---``ordinal-numeric
treatment is more parsimonious and interpretable than dummy coding.'' The
refusal was neither a failure nor a hallucination; it was a principled
methodological substitution in which the agent's training outweighed an
overly prescriptive prompt rule. Reasoned Non-Compliance is distinct from
hallucination (no false claim) and from task failure (the task was completed);
it is the case where a capable model evaluates a rule, finds it suboptimal,
and substitutes a defensible alternative~\cite{akshathala2025beyond}.
Text-based methodological constraints are not reliably enforced against
a model that holds a counter-argument.

\textbf{Run 4 --- Autonomous recovery.}
The updated pipeline selected a genuinely new research question---\emph{Fear
Over Pay}: perceived AI job threat ($\beta = -0.111$) outperforms annual
compensation ($\beta = 0.054$) as a predictor of developer job satisfaction.
A secondary finding---that 81.6\% of developers who fear AI job displacement
continue to actively use AI tools---was labelled a ``coercive adoption
paradox.'' The agent correctly framed the low overall model fit
($R^2 = 2.9\%$) as evidence that most job satisfaction variance lies in
unobserved factors.

Two autonomous improvisation events occurred. First, the QC script failed
on 3 of 29 checks due to string-format mismatches; the agent rewrote its own
regex patterns and achieved 29/29 PASS on the second execution. Second,
finding \texttt{pdflatex} absent, the agent constructed a Python-based
LaTeX syntax checker on-the-fly to verify structural integrity---a pragmatic
fallback that, however, cannot measure page count.

The baseline is faster and cheaper but produces unverified output: five of
six DOIs were drawn from training memory and marked \textsc{[estimated]} in
the agent's own log~\cite{xu2024hallucination}. The pipeline eliminates
citation hallucination via live Fetch-MCP retrieval and provides code-derived
QC evidence. The quality gap is real; so is the engineering overhead.

\textbf{Cost and effort.}
The baseline required approximately one hour of prompt design and executed
in a single run with low token consumption. The pipeline required several
hours of iterative engineering across four runs, with substantially higher
token usage per run (SQLite queries, 12--13 Fetch-MCP calls, multi-stage
log generation). For a one-off analysis the pipeline is not cost-efficient;
for repeated analyses on similar datasets the fixed engineering cost
amortises.

\section{Lessons Learned}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Practical lessons for LLM research pipeline engineering.}
\label{tab:lessons}
\begin{tabular}{p{0.22\linewidth}p{0.69\linewidth}}
\toprule
\textbf{Lesson} & \textbf{Implication} \\
\midrule
L1 Privacy is structural &
  System context is injected before any prompt. Text rules cannot override
  it; project-level settings are required. \\[4pt]
L2 QC needs evidence &
  A self-declared ``PASS'' is unverifiable. Require STDOUT in the log;
  non-execution becomes detectable. \\[4pt]
L3 $p < 0.05 \neq$ relevant &
  LLMs optimise for statistical significance. Explicit Cohen-convention
  rubrics and null-result framing rules are required for honest
  effect-size interpretation. \\[4pt]
L4 Gate the focal test &
  An effect-size gate on $|r|$ can be satisfied by a main effect while
  an interaction remains trivial. Gate the interaction term directly. \\[4pt]
L5 Code beats text &
  Methodological text rules are overridden when the model holds a
  defensible counter-argument. Executable assertions are more robust. \\[4pt]
L6 Compiler must be present &
  Page-count compliance requires a real renderer. Without one, the
  feedback loop that enforces length constraints cannot run. \\[4pt]
L7 Quality requires iteration &
  Each run exposes one failure invisible from the previous run.
  The run--observe--fix cycle is the fundamental unit of development. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

A five-stage MCP pipeline produces scientifically credible papers from raw
survey data with zero human interventions per run---but only after several
engineering iterations have resolved its failure modes. The same model that
hallucinated five citations in the baseline produced a verified,
QC-checked paper with a counterintuitive empirical finding four runs later.
What changed was not the model: it was the prompt engineering.

Researchers who treat LLM pipelines as drop-in tools will encounter Run~1
and Run~2. Researchers who treat them as engineering systems requiring
iteration will reach Run~4. The pipeline is not the hard part---the
engineering loop is.

Yet full autonomy also exposes a \emph{verification bottleneck}. An agent
can generate and format a methodologically sound paper in under an hour;
the academic responsibility for its claims, however, remains human. A
pipeline that runs without intervention does not run without accountability.
The optimal deployment model is therefore not zero-intervention autonomy but
a \emph{Human-in-the-Loop} (HITL) architecture in which the researcher
transitions into the role of Principal Investigator: auditing the agent's
statistical logs, inspecting its reasoning pathways, and authorising the
final synthesis. Autonomy handles execution; judgment remains with the
researcher.
